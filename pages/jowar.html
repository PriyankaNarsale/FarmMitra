Jowar (Sorghum bicolor) is a major cereal and fodder crop cultivated extensively in rainfed and dryland areas. It grows best in warm, dry climates with temperatures between 25‚Äì35¬∞C. Major producing states are Maharashtra, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan and Gujarat.

Jowar is used as grain for human food, cattle feed, silage and green fodder. Its deep root system and waxy leaves help it tolerate drought and high temperatures better than many other cereals.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Soil Preparation & Sowing

Jowar is generally direct sown in the main field. The land should be well prepared, free from weeds and with proper tilth for good seed-soil contact.

Land Preparation
  • 1‚Äì2 deep ploughings followed by 2‚Äì3 harrowings.
  • Break clods, level the land and remove weeds/stubbles.
  • Apply FYM or compost and mix thoroughly in soil.
Sowing Method
  • Line sowing with seed drill / behind country plough.
  • Spacing: 45 cm between rows and 15 cm between plants.
  • Depth of sowing: about 3‚Äì4 cm.
Thinning & Gap Filling
  • Thin excess plants at 2‚Äì3 leaf stage to maintain spacing.
  • Fill gaps using extra seedlings or re-sowing where plant stand is poor.
Intercropping Option
  • Can be intercropped with pulses (red gram, cowpea).
  • Improves soil fertility and spreads risk under rainfed conditions.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Water Management & Weed Control

Water Management
  • Under rainfed, sow with onset of monsoon and conserve soil moisture.
  • In irrigated crop, give light irrigations at tillering, flowering and grain filling.
  • Avoid waterlogging ‚Äì Jowar does not like standing water.
Weed Management
  • Critical period: first 3‚Äì5 weeks after sowing.
  • Hand/hoe weeding at 15‚Äì20 and 30‚Äì35 days after sowing.
  • Mulching or suitable herbicides as per local recommendations can also be used.
üå± Tip: Early weeding and one or two intercultivations not only control weeds but also help in soil aeration and moisture conservation in Jowar fields.

‚ÄúDead heart‚Äù in young seedlings
  • Delay sowing increases damage ‚Äì sow at optimum time.
  • Use recommended seed treatment and resistant varieties.

Stem Borer

Shot holes on leaves, tunnelling of stem
  • Destroy stubbles and crop residues after harvest.
  • Need-based sprays/traps as per expert advice.

Grain Mold

Discoloured, shrivelled grains in humid weather
  • Harvest at proper maturity, avoid very late harvest.
  • Dry grain properly and store in dry conditions.

Anthracnose / Leaf Blight

Spots and blight on leaves
  • Use disease tolerant varieties and crop rotation.
  • Follow balanced nutrition and avoid excess nitrogen.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Farmer FAQ ‚Äì Jowar Cultivation

Q1. What spacing is best for Jowar? ‚Ä∫

Maintain about 45 cm row to row and 15 cm plant to plant distance for grain Jowar. This gives good aeration, better tillering and easier interculture.

Q2. How many irrigations are needed in Jowar? ‚Ä∫

Under irrigated conditions, give light irrigations at tillering, flowering and grain filling. In rainfed areas, timely sowing and moisture conservation are most important.

Q3. Can Jowar be used as fodder? ‚Ä∫

Yes, many Jowar varieties are excellent green fodder and silage crops. Multicut fodder sorghums (SSG types) can be harvested several times for high-quality green fodder.

Q4. Is Jowar suitable for intercropping? ‚Ä∫

Jowar is commonly intercropped with pulses like red gram, cowpea or green gram. It improves overall system productivity and reduces risk in rainfed farming.
